Moving to Linux: My New Dev Setup

For a long time, Windows was my default environment. It was where I wrote my first lines of HTML, CSS, and JavaScript. But as I leaned deeper into web and game development, the bloat, the forced updates, and the clunky command-line experience started to feel like anchor weights on my workflow.

I needed an OS that felt as clean, fast, and customizable as the code I was trying to write. That’s when I decided to wipe the drive on my Fujitsu LIFEBOOK and install Zorin OS.



Why Zorin OS?

I wanted a Linux distribution that didn't require a computer science degree just to configure the desktop, but still gave me full terminal power. Zorin OS was the perfect middle ground. It’s based on Ubuntu, meaning it’s incredibly stable and has massive community support, but its interface is beautifully polished right out of the box—minimal, dark, and lightning-fast on older hardware.



The Installation Hurdles (Looking at you, GRUB)

Let’s be honest: switching to Linux is rarely a "one-click" story. After flashing my bootable USB drive using Ventoy, I ran into my first real test—the infamous GRUB bootloader errors.

My laptop's UEFI settings and the existing Windows partitions did not want to cooperate. Instead of a clean boot, I was greeted with a minimal GRUB rescue prompt.



How I fixed it:

Instead of panicking, I booted back into the live USB, opened up the Disks utility, and manually wiped the drive's partition table. I allocated:

• A dedicated EFI system partition for the bootloader.
• A massive root (/) partition formatted to ext4 for the OS and my projects.

Once the partition hierarchy was clean, the installer went through flawlessly.



Crafting the Dev Environment

Once I logged in, the first thing I did was purge the stock apps and open the terminal. A developer’s terminal is their home, so I spent time customizing it to match my aesthetic.

1. Terminal Customization: To make the environment truly mine, I installed Fastfetch. Now, every time I pop open the terminal, I get a clean, customized system overview tracking my hardware, uptime, and shell configuration.

2. The Godot Factor: Setting up the Godot Engine for my game development projects on Linux was a breath of fresh air. Performance feels snappier, and running scripts from the terminal just works without path configuration headaches.
